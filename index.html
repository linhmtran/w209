California Traffic Accidents of 2017
California Traffic Accidents of 2017
As unfortunate as it may be, traffic collisions are an inevitable occurence in the world of commuting and California is in no short supply of commuters or car collisions. These visualizations help us explore different facets of traffic collisions within the state of California. The dataset was taken from all vehicle collisions reported to CHP and other law enforcement agencies for the entire year of 2017. Dive through and explore the data for your region.

Start Exploring

WHERE ARE YOU DRIVING?

Where you are located or where you drive can affect the odds of your involvment in a traffic collision as well as what type of collision you may be involved in.

The map visualization allows you not only locate your region of driving, but it also provides some insights into collision numbers, providing data on raw number of collisions as well as number of collisions per 10,000 population. Each county is colored: darker the shade, the higher number of collisions there are relative to other counties in the state. Use the radio buttons to change the view to display the number of collisions per 10,000 population.

Some interesting takeaways:
  • Los Angeles has by far the highest number of traffic collisions.
  • Alpine county has the highest number of collisions per 10,000 population.

    • This makes some sense if you know the areas. Los Angeles has a huge population and strong car culture. Known for densely populated area, an impressive highway system, and many commuters, the sheer number of drivers alongside you raises the potential for a traffic collision. However, with respect to its population size, its collision rate is not far off from other counties in the state.

    Alpine county on the other hand, is a rather remote region near Lake Tahoe, it is someone secluded, with rugged terrain, mountains and weather factors. Many travelers will road trip through this area. It makes sense sense they have a very low number of collisions, but due to travlers, they have a high number of collisions for their population size.

    Use the map visualization to explore at a high level the number and rate of traffic collisions for your county or others. This information will cross filter the other visualizations so you can learn about different areas in further detail. What do you find interesting in your region?

    WHEN ARE YOU DRIVING?

    When you are on the road also influence your propensity for being involved in a traffic collision. Try exploring the collision patterns across various counties to see if there are any differences in time of day and day of week between regions.

    Some interesting takeaways:
  • Collision counts peak around morning 7-8AM and afternoons 4-6PM, with Fridays generally having the greatest concentration of accidents for most counties.
  • For less populated areas or areas away from main cities, we see accidents occuring more sporadically throughout the days of week or times of days (such as Inyo, Mono, etc.)

    • During rush-hour times, more cars are on the road competeing for space and time in some instances which can lead to tumultous and unfortunate situations. This is not surprising, since more cars on the road will result in a higher accident rate. Being aware of these conditions can help you as a driver!

    Traffic and collision patterns share common traits throughout the week, but it's also interesting to see how different days of the week may vary in collision patterns. For instance, Friday evenings tend to have a greater number of collisions as opposed to the other days of weeks. If Tuesday home-bound commutes are the same times as Friday home-bound commutes, what are potential reasons that Fridays having a greater volume of collisions? Could it be behavioral (everyone is more eager and aggressive to go home)? Or could it be that the spread of commute times is smaller on Fridays as compared to other days?

    WHAT ARE THE FACTORS?

    What are the causes of collisions and what are the consequences?

    Although the majority of collisions only result in property damage, there are many incidents that result in injury or worse, fatality. Explore some of the causes of collisions in your county and what proportion of them result in injury or death. Understanding these factors can help inform you about the types of things to be on the look-out for in your area to avoid becoming a collision statistic.

    Based on the county selected in a previous section, the bar chart will update to show the number of collisions for each CHP Primary Collision Factor violation category. This can be speeding, brake failures, driving under the influence (DUI). Although, many counties seem to share a consistent pattern of violations: travelling at an unsafe speed is almost always a clear winner! The bar chart on the right will initially show the injury and fatality rates across all collisions in the selected county. To see the injury and fatality rate for a specific CHP Primary Collision Factor, select the corresponding bar in the chart on the left. This selection will automatically cause the right-side bar chart to show the injury and fatality rates for the selected collision factor.

    Please note the changing scale based on the county selection. Naturally, smaller populous regions have few collisions on records. Take a moment to explore your region and others! See what violaions are most prevalent in your area and discover things you should be wary of to help become a safer driver.

    Some interesting takeaways:
  • Incidents that involve pedestrians have a much higher injury and fatality rate, 32X higher fatality rate than the countywide fatality rate in LA County
  • Collisions caused by drunk drivers or that involve a vehicle travelling the wrong way also cause injury and fatality rates higher than the countywide injury and fatality rates

    • It doesn't take an engineer to know that pedestrians don't stand a chance against a 3,000 lb vehicle travelling at 40 mph. Always be alert and on the lookout for pedestrians and bicyclists, especially in congested areas. Even a slow speed collision with someone who isn't protected by the armor of a vehicle (like a pedestrian or bicyclist) can cause debilitating injuries or death.

    To minimize the risk of a run-in with a drunk driver, consider the time of day you decide to drive. Your rush hour commute in the mornings and evenings may not put you at increased risk, but being on the road at 1 AM on New Year's Day definitely will.
